# Reading a briefing

You've run the pipeline and a briefing has rendered. Now: what are you actually looking at?

A briefing is a **structured reading view**, not a paper list. It's designed around one question: which 1-3 papers should you actually open today, and which can you skip confidently? Everything below exists to answer that question quickly.

This page walks through the briefing top-to-bottom, explains the two ways to dig into a specific paper, and then covers two pieces of out-of-band context — the hallucination audit and the generation provenance — that live beneath the briefing itself.

## The three sections

A briefing has exactly three sections. In order:

### 1. Executive summary

The first block on the page — 2-4 paragraphs, roughly 200-400 words, in serif prose.

This is the editorial lead. It frames the day's papers as a story rather than a list. Read it before anything else.

- **First paragraph — the headline.** What's the single most important thing in today's papers? If you starred papers in previous runs, those anchor the framing.
- **Middle paragraphs — the thematic structure.** What clusters emerged? What's the throughline? If the day is scattered, the summary will say so honestly rather than invent coherence.
- **Final paragraph — a reading recommendation.** "If you read one paper today, make it \[arxivId\] because..." — this is the most actionable sentence in the briefing.

If you're busy, reading just the executive summary is often enough. It's designed to be skimmable in under two minutes.

### 2. Themes

Next comes 2-5 **theme sections**. Each theme is a priority-ordered grouping of papers that share an argument, method, problem, or tension.

Each theme has:

- **A numbered heading**, e.g. "1. Interpretability converges on attention heads." Theme titles are meant to read as arguments or observations, not category labels.
- **A 2-4 sentence argument** in italic serif, explaining why the papers below belong together and what the takeaway is. If papers within a theme are in tension or build on each other, the argument will say so — debates live inside themes, not in a separate section.
- **A list of paper cards** — the papers assigned to this theme (see below).

Themes are priority-ordered: the most important theme (containing the highest-scored and starred papers) comes first. A paper can appear in only one theme; every paper appears somewhere.

### 3. Paper cards

Each theme contains one or more paper cards. A card gives you everything you need to decide whether to open the actual PDF:

- **Score badge** — a 0-10 relevance score (e.g. "9.2"). The badge is highlighted more brightly for scores ≥ 9.
- **Title** — the paper's full title, linking to arxiv.org.
- **ArXiv ID** — the arXiv identifier, linked to the abstract page.
- **One-line pitch** — a 15-25 word italic sell of the paper's argument or contribution. Not a summary; a pitch. "What would this paper say if it were selling itself in one sentence?"
- **Why it matters** — a 2-4 sentence paragraph grounded in *your profile* and your *engagement signals*. This is where starred papers get the richest treatment; dismissed papers get short, honest acknowledgement; and normal papers get a relevance argument against your stated interests.
- **Action buttons** — `→ quick summary`, `→ full report`, `☆ star`, `⊘ dismiss`, `+ comment`. The first two expand content (see below); the last three are feedback (see [Giving feedback](/using/giving-feedback)).

## The two expansions

Paper cards are deliberately short — they're meant to help you triage, not replace reading. When you want more depth on a specific paper, there are two ways to go deeper:

### Quick summary (inline expansion)

Click `→ quick summary` on any paper card. An inline block expands directly beneath the card, showing a roughly 300-word compression of the paper's content — enough to understand the contribution, the method, and the result.

Quick summaries are generated by a fast, cheap model (defaults to the same model as PDF analysis) and run in parallel during briefing synthesis. They're intermediate-depth: richer than a pitch, lighter than the full report. Click again to collapse.

### Full report (side panel)

Click `→ full report` to open a side panel (about 55% of the viewport width, sliding in from the right). This shows the full per-paper technical report generated during Stage 3 (PDF analysis) — often 1000+ words of summary, key findings, methodology notes, and limitations.

Use the full report when you want to understand a paper deeply without opening the actual PDF. Close it by clicking the `X` button or clicking outside the panel.

Neither expansion costs you anything at read time — both contents are generated once during the pipeline run and cached.

## Hallucination audit disclosure

Below the briefing body, you'll see a card with a **verdict pill** and (sometimes) a disclosure labelled "flagged claim(s) · click to view."

This is the **hallucination audit**. After synthesis, Aparture runs a second LLM pass that audits the briefing's claims against the source papers. The verdict is:

- **NO** (green pill) — no hallucinations detected. The briefing's claims are supported by the source material. This is what you want to see most of the time.
- **MAYBE** (amber pill) — uncertain. Some claims could be reasonable inferences, but the audit couldn't verify them confidently. Worth a skim of the flagged claims.
- **YES** (red pill) — the audit found claims that don't appear to be supported by the source papers. Read the flagged claims before trusting the briefing's prose.

If `briefingRetryOnYes` or `briefingRetryOnMaybe` is enabled in Settings (both default on), Aparture will automatically retry synthesis with a hint about the failed audit. When that happens, the verdict pill shows "(after retry)" — meaning the briefing you're reading is the second attempt, and the audit ran again on it.

The flagged-claims list shows each problematic excerpt, the paper it was about (by arXiv ID), and what the auditor's concern was. You can usually tell at a glance whether the flagged claim is a real hallucination or a reasonable paraphrase the auditor was too strict about.

**When to worry:** YES verdicts on major claims in the executive summary. Those affect your reading decisions. MAYBE verdicts on specific paper details are usually fine — the underlying papers are still the ground truth; the briefing is a reading aid.

## Generation provenance disclosure

Below the briefing (and the hallucination card) is a small collapsible link: **"Generation details ▸"**. Click it to expand.

This shows the full provenance of the briefing — everything that went into generating it:

- **Profile snapshot** — the exact profile text that was in effect when the briefing was generated (truncated at 200 chars, expandable). Useful when you come back to an old briefing and wonder "why did it rank things this way?".
- **Models** — the specific model IDs used at each pipeline stage (filter, scoring, PDF, briefing) in monospace.
- **Categories** — the arXiv categories selected for this run, shown as pill badges.
- **Filter verdicts** — the YES/MAYBE/NO counts from the quick-filter stage.
- **Settings flags** — whether pause gates and retry checks were on or off.
- **Hallucination audit** — the same verdict and flagged-claims list as above, plus the audit's justification prose.
- **Generated at** — ISO timestamp.

You'll rarely need this on a fresh briefing. It's most useful weeks later when you're reviewing old briefings and trying to understand what you were seeing at the time — which is also why briefings are archived for 90 days in the sidebar.

## Triage patterns

With the briefing in front of you, you're deciding what to do with each paper. Three patterns cover the common cases:

### Read now

Papers with score ≥ 9 and a strong "why it matters" paragraph, especially ones at the top of theme 1. Open the full report side panel; if the report sells you, open the PDF.

### Star for later

Papers you want to come back to but don't have time for now. Click `☆ star`. The star persists across runs and will anchor future briefings around related topics — starred papers get the richest treatment in the synthesis prompt.

Starring is cheap. Err on the side of starring anything that catches your eye; you can always unstar later.

### Dismiss

Papers you don't want to see again, especially ones Aparture keeps surfacing even though they're not a fit. Click `⊘ dismiss`. Dismissed papers get deprioritized in future briefings and feed into Suggest-Improvements as "the profile may be too broad on this dimension" signals.

Be deliberate about dismissing. A dismiss is a strong signal — it tells the system "stop bringing me these" — so use it when a paper is genuinely not what you want, not just when you're too tired to read it today.

For comments and the other feedback types, see [Giving feedback](/using/giving-feedback).
